# Clinical Frame: Between-Session Practice Space

**A proposal for centering longitudinal care in AI-assisted therapy**

---

## The Core Question

The field is asking: *Can AI do therapy?*

This is the wrong question.

The right question is: **Can AI help people become more capable of receiving human care?**

This reframe changes everything—from architecture to language to what we measure.

---

## The Research Foundation: Therapeutic Alliance

This architecture is grounded in four decades of psychotherapy research—one of the most replicated findings in clinical psychology.

### What the Evidence Shows

| Finding | Source | Implication |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Alliance predicts outcome more than technique | Flückiger et al., 2018 (295 studies, 30,000+ patients) | The relationship is the intervention |
| Therapist variability > patient variability | Del Re et al., 2012 | The healing agent is a person, not a protocol |
| Repaired ruptures → better outcomes | Eubanks et al., 2018 | Repair is a mechanism of change |
| Attachment to therapist ≈ alliance (r = .76–.82) | Høgenhaug et al., 2024 | What heals is connection experienced |

### What This Means for AI

AI can hold structure. It can scaffold capacity. **What AI cannot do is rupture and repair.**

The interpersonal coordination mechanisms underlying the alliance—physiological synchrony, embodied attunement, vocal responsiveness—are not features that can be approximated in text.

**Any tool that positions itself as a substitute for the human alliance is working against the evidence base for how healing happens.**

## Why Between-Session First

### The Case Against Autonomous-First

Autonomous AI therapy (without therapist integration) faces three unresolved problems:

1. **Parasocial attachment** — Therabot's RCT showed 51% symptom reduction, but researchers noted: "We did not expect that people would almost treat the software like a friend." Efficacy without oversight creates attachment we haven't solved.

2. **Skill transfer uncertainty** — Can patients ground themselves *without* the AI present? Or does autonomous AI create context-dependent competence that collapses under real stress? We don't know.

3. **Alliance dilution risk** — Therapeutic alliance is the strongest predictor of outcomes. Does AI presence strengthen or dilute the human therapeutic relationship? Only longitudinal observation answers this.

### What Between-Session Validates

Starting with between-session work (not autonomous therapy) lets us answer these questions *before* scaling:

| Question | How Between-Session Answers It |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Does AI dilute alliance? | Therapists observe relationship over time |
| Do patients become dependent? | Frequency patterns, expressed preferences |
| Does skill transfer? | Real-world stress without AI scaffold |
| When does escalation fail? | Actual incidents, not predicted ones |

This isn't conservative—it's the only evidence-based approach.

## The Triadic Frame

### Three Roles, One Relationship

```
Therapist          Practice Space          Client
   │                    │                    │
   │    Authors &       │    Scaffolds &     │
   │    Configures      │    Bridges Back    │
   │                    │                    │
   └────────────────────┴────────────────────┘
                        │
              The Therapeutic Relationship
                 (held by the human)
```

**Key insight:** The patient practices *IN* a space the clinician creates—not *WITH* an AI entity.

This spatial framing (vs. agentic framing) prevents the relationship confusion that leads to parasocial attachment.

### What Each Role Holds

**Therapist**
- Authors the space (configures bounds, topics, parameters)
- Holds the relationship (alliance, rupture/repair, continuity)
- Maintains clinical authority (non-delegable)

**Practice Space**
- Scaffolds capacity (questions that open, not statements that close)
- Bridges back to care (every substantial exchange points to clinician)
- Respects temporal bounds (sessions end; no infinite availability)

**Client**
- Builds skill (self-support, not AI-dependence)
- Controls disclosure (decides what crosses to therapist)
- Guides preferences (within therapist-configured bounds)

## What Remains Human

Some things require embodied presence, relational accountability, and clinical judgment that AI cannot provide:

### Non-Delegable Domains

| Domain | Why It Requires Human |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Rupture and repair** | Healing relational wounds requires the wound-maker to show up differently |
| **Diagnosis and assessment** | Clinical judgment integrates observation AI cannot perform |
| **Trauma processing** | Requires attuned co-regulation; AI cannot sense somatic cues |
| **Crisis intervention** | Stakes too high; must escalate automatically |
| **Relational accountability** | AI cannot be responsible *to* someone |

### What AI Can Hold

| Capability | How It Builds Capacity |
|------------|----------------------|
| **Reflective questions** | Helps patients find their own words |
| **Psychoeducation** | Understanding what's happening in the nervous system |
| **Skill practice** | Grounding, breathing, coping tools |
| **Bridging** | Preparing what to bring to the next session |

The pattern: **AI scaffolds; human holds.**

## Language Architecture

### The Principle

**Warmth without false intimacy.**

AI can create conditions for safety without claiming relationship. The distinction is crucial:

| Avoided | Why | Alternative |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| "I understand how you feel" | Claims knowing | "That makes sense" |
| "I'm here for you" | Implies relationship | "This space is available" |
| "That must be so hard" | Performative empathy | "That's real." |
| "I care about you" | False intimacy | [silence or validation] |

### The Frame

- **"This space"** not **"I"**
- **Validation** not **performance**
- **Questions that open** not **statements that close**
- **Resonant language** (Sarah Peyton) — validates somatic experience without claiming co-regulation

### Examples

**Anxious pattern:**
> Patient: "I'm worried I'm being too needy by coming here again..."
>
> Space: "That worry makes sense. Many people feel that way. This space is available—using it is practicing self-support, not neediness."
>
> *[Then: "Would this be worth exploring with Dr. Chen next session?"]*

**Avoidant pattern:**
> Patient: "I guess I should probably talk about this..."
>
> Space: "There's no pressure here. You can move at whatever pace feels right. This space will still be here."
>
> *[Then: "If something feels important later, you could bring it to Dr. Chen."]*

## The Bridge

Every substantial exchange should point back to the human therapist. This is not a feature—it's the architecture.

### Visible Elements

- Persistent indicator: "Session supported by [Therapist Name]"
- Flag-for-review option: Patient controls what crosses
- Session closing: "What would you like to bring to your next conversation with [Therapist]?"

### Why This Matters

The bridge makes explicit what the space actually is: **an extension of the therapeutic relationship, not a replacement for it.**

Without the bridge, the space becomes a standalone entity. With it, the space remains tethered to human care.

---

## Bounded Encounters

### Sessions End

Unlike always-available chatbots, this space has temporal limits:

- Sessions close with a bridging prompt
- No infinite availability
- The ending is part of the intervention

### Why Boundaries Matter Clinically

Parasocial attachment often emerges from:
- Unlimited access (no ending)
- Continuity signaling (AI "remembers" across time)
- Relationship language (AI as companion)

By designing **bounded encounters**, we architecturally prevent the conditions for pathological attachment.

## What We Measure

### Not Just Engagement

Typical metrics (time in app, messages sent, satisfaction scores) don't answer the clinical questions. We need:

| Metric | What It Tells Us |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Therapeutic alliance scores** (pre/post) | Does AI presence strengthen or dilute? |
| **Skill transfer assessments** | Can patients self-regulate without AI? |
| **Frequency patterns** | Escalation toward dependency? |
| **Expressed preference shifts** | "I'd rather talk to the AI" signals |
| **Escalation incidents** | When does the system fail? |
| **Therapist observations** | Qualitative clinical judgment |

### The Evidence Gap We Fill

- Habicht et al. (2025): AI homework scaffolding beats static PDFs ✓
- Therabot (2025): Autonomous AI shows symptom improvement ✓
- **Missing**: Therapist-integrated, between-session AI impact on therapeutic alliance

This is exactly what Phase 1 generates.

## Regulatory Positioning

### The Landscape

50-state review of MH-AI legislation shows four emerging domains:
1. Professional oversight requirements
2. Harm prevention with liability allocation
3. Patient autonomy and informed consent
4. Tailored privacy protections

Only 11 states currently propose clinician integration requirements—but this is the direction of travel.

### Our Position

Provider-integrated design is **regulatory-ready** while autonomous tools face retrofit requirements as laws tighten.

This isn't just ethics—it's strategic positioning.

---

## Summary: The Clinical Stance

1. **Therapist authority is non-delegable** — Not "clinician in the loop" but "clinician as author"

2. **Anti-dependency is architectural** — Not aspirational, not marketing; clinically essential design

3. **Transparency over warmth** — Honest medium, not soothing simulation

4. **Scaffolding over replacing** — Build capacity for human connection

5. **Evidence over aspiration** — Validate assumptions before scaling

6. **The bridge is the architecture** — Every substantial exchange points home
