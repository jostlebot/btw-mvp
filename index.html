The Evidence Base

This architecture is grounded in four decades of psychotherapy research.

The core finding: The quality of the therapist-client relationship predicts treatment outcome more reliably than technique, modality, or diagnosis. This is one of the most replicated findings in clinical psychology.

The Therapist Is the Variable That Matters

Who the therapist is matters more than who the patient is.

Key finding: Therapist variability accounts for more variance in the alliance-outcome relationship than patient variability (Del Re et al., 2012). The healing agent is not simply a protocol — it is a person.
Design implication: The therapist must author the AI space — configuring it to their clinical approach, their knowledge of this patient, their judgment. AI cannot substitute for this.

Rupture and Repair: Where Healing Lives

It's not the absence of problems — it's how they're repaired.

The research: Treatment including repaired ruptures is associated with better outcomes than treatment without ruptures at all (Eubanks et al., 2018). The repair itself — the therapist's willingness to acknowledge, attune, and re-engage — is a mechanism of change.
What this requires: A human who can be genuinely impacted, genuinely wrong, and genuinely present enough to acknowledge it. AI cannot rupture or repair.

What This Means for AI

AI extends reach. It does not replace relationship.

✓

✓

✗

✗
AI cannot replace the alliance — working against the evidence base for how healing happens
The position: Any tool that positions itself as a substitute for the human alliance is not just clinically unsupported — it is working against the evidence base for how healing happens.
